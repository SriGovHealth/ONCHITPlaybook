Technology has changed how patients monitor their own health.

1 in 5 people use technology to track their health — from fitness monitors to home medical devices.

But the potential medical benefits of collecting that data are often lost — because clinicians aren’t able to use it to improve their patients’ care.

Clinicians usually can’t access that data. And even if they could, they just don’t have the time to sort through it all on their own.

• • • • • •

But some clinics are finding innovative ways to make this data genuinely useful. They’re pulling it into their EHRs, where it can be organized and visualized.

[Image: Screen showing medical info in an EHR]

Using patient-generated health data has big advantages. Studies show it:

  • Improves patient engagement and self-management skills
  • Lowers hospital readmission rates
  • Improves outcomes
  • Is more accurate than patient reporting

As EHRs become capable of integrating more patient-generated health data, the potential benefits are huge.

[Image: Silhouette of patient with clickable hotspots. Clicking one brings up a light box with the copy below.]

Previous Next
• • • • • •

[Image: Activity tracker on patient’s wrist]

Activity trackers don’t just count steps. You could use the data to assess sleep quality or make sure a patient is getting up and moving around after surgery.

[Image: Home blood pressure monitor on a nearby table]

Home blood pressure monitors let you track the effects of a new medication remotely — or rule out white coat hypertension.

[Image: Phone in patient’s hand]

Food and symptom diaries can help you manage chronic diseases and identify triggers.

[Image: Glucometer on a nearby table]

Home glucometers that send automated updates avoid the hassle of typing in blood sugar readings — and offer access to a deeper level of data.
